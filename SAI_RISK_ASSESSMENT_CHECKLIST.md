# SAI Risk Assessment Checklist

## System-Level Questions Before Any Stratospheric Aerosol Injection Deployment

This checklist is designed to prevent Stratospheric Aerosol Injection (SAI) from being evaluated only as a sunlight-reflection technique.

SAI must be assessed as a large-scale intervention into the atmospheric particle system, hydrological cycle, wet deposition, surface particle fixation, cloud formation, rainfall, regional climate, agriculture, health, ocean systems, governance, and termination risk.

---

## 1. Existing Atmospheric Particle Inventory

Before adding artificial aerosols, the existing atmospheric particle environment must be assessed.

```text
Which particles already exist in the atmosphere?
At what altitudes are they distributed?
What are their sizes, chemical compositions, colors, and optical properties?
Which particles scatter sunlight?
Which particles absorb heat?
Which particles interact with clouds and rainfall?
Which particles are natural, anthropogenic, mixed, or insufficiently classified?
```

Required particle categories include:

```text
sulfate aerosols
desert dust
Asian dust
fine road dust
smoke
soot / black carbon
sea salt
pollen
spores
biogenic particles
combustion-derived particles
PM2.5
mixed mineral-organic particles
unclassified complex aerosols
```

---

## 2. Altitude and Layering Assessment

SAI cannot be evaluated only by total aerosol mass.

Altitude matters.

```text
Which particles are in the boundary layer?
Which particles are in the free troposphere?
Which particles are in the upper troposphere?
Which particles already reach the lower stratosphere?
How do existing particle layers interact with proposed SAI layers?
Could additional aerosol layers alter heat trapping, cloud formation, or atmospheric circulation?
```

A valid SAI assessment must evaluate vertical particle structure, not merely global average aerosol optical depth.

---

## 3. Wet Deposition and Rain-Cleaning Capacity

Rain is Earth's atmospheric cleaning system.

Before SAI is considered, the following must be assessed:

```text
Is rainfall still effectively removing particles from the atmosphere?
Where has rainfall become localized or weakened?
Where have dry periods increased?
How has wet deposition changed regionally?
Which particles are no longer being washed out efficiently?
How much atmospheric cleaning capacity has been lost due to hydrological disruption?
```

SAI should not be assessed independently from rainfall and wet deposition.

---

## 4. Surface Fixation and Natural Particle Traps

Particles removed from the atmosphere must be captured by the surface system.

The following natural traps must be evaluated:

```text
moist soils
humus-rich soils
wetlands
forests
grasslands
rivers
lakes
oceans
microbially active soil structures
plant root systems
forest leaf surfaces
```

If these systems are degraded, deposited particles may return to the air.

---

## 5. Resuspension Risk

Dry surfaces can re-lift deposited particles.

SAI assessment must include:

```text
soil dryness
bare land expansion
desertification
urban dust loading
road dust resuspension
vehicle-induced turbulence
wind erosion
surface heating
dry agricultural fields
loss of vegetation cover
```

A planet with increasing resuspension risk should not be treated as an empty atmosphere ready for more particles.

---

## 6. Radiation Balance Beyond Incoming Sunlight

SAI is often justified as a solar-reflection method.

However, incoming sunlight is only one side of the planetary heat balance.

Assessment must include:

```text
incoming shortwave radiation
outgoing longwave radiation
particle absorption
particle scattering
cloud optical effects
infrared trapping and re-radiation
surface albedo interactions
water vapor and cloud feedbacks
latent heat transport
evapotranspiration changes
```

SAI should not be evaluated as a simple planetary umbrella.

---

## 7. Water-Cycle and Natural Cooling Feedback Risk

True cooling depends on water circulation.

SAI risk evaluation must include possible impacts on:

```text
evapotranspiration
cloud formation
rainfall distribution
monsoon systems
regional drought
soil moisture
wetland recovery
river flows
ocean-atmosphere exchange
natural cooling feedbacks
```

Any intervention that weakens these systems may reduce sunlight while worsening cooling-system failure.

---

## 8. Agriculture, Health, and Ecosystem Effects

SAI must be assessed beyond global mean temperature.

Required impact categories include:

```text
crop yield changes
sunlight reduction for photosynthesis
regional precipitation shifts
human respiratory health
PM exposure interactions
acid deposition risks
ocean biogeochemistry
ecosystem stress
pollinator and pollen interactions
fishery impacts
food security
```

Global average cooling does not guarantee regional safety.

---

## 9. Governance and Consent

SAI affects the entire planet.

Governance assessment must include:

```text
who decides deployment
who controls dosage
who monitors side effects
who compensates harmed regions
who can stop the project
how regional climate disputes are handled
how long deployment continues
how future generations are bound by the decision
```

A technology that affects global rainfall and radiation cannot be governed as a narrow technical experiment.

---

## 10. Termination Shock Risk

If SAI is started and then suddenly stopped, suppressed warming may rapidly reappear.

Assessment must include:

```text
rate of temperature rebound after termination
regional climate shock
agricultural adaptation failure
ecosystem stress
political instability
long-term dependency risk
financial continuity risk
military or geopolitical disruption risk
```

A system that requires indefinite continuation is not a stable restoration strategy.

---

## 11. Cooling Credit Eligibility Test

Under the Cooling Credit framework, SAI should fail unless it can demonstrate restoration of natural cooling feedbacks.

The test is:

```text
Does it restore water circulation?
Does it restore soil moisture?
Does it restore evapotranspiration?
Does it restore cloud-rainfall cycles?
Does it restore wet deposition and atmospheric cleaning?
Does it restore surface fixation of particles?
Does it regenerate forests, wetlands, rivers, soils, or oceans?
Does it reduce particle resuspension?
Does it restart natural cooling feedbacks?
```

If the answer is no, then SAI is shading, not cooling.

---

## Summary Decision Rule

SAI should not be treated as a valid climate-cooling intervention unless it passes a full system assessment covering:

```text
existing atmospheric particles
vertical aerosol layering
wet deposition
surface fixation
resuspension risk
cloud and rainfall impacts
outgoing heat release
water-cycle feedbacks
regional risks
governance
termination shock
natural cooling restoration
```

Without this assessment, adding aerosols is not a climate solution.

It is an additional intervention into an already stressed planetary system.

---

## Core Principle

> Shading is not cooling.  
> Cooling means restoring planetary circulation.
